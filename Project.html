Data Science Project

Are low-cost carriers (LCCs) taking over the European airline market?

The main aim is to examine the changing European airline market by anayzing data on the largest carriers and airline groups based in Europe, including the demand for those.

Passenger growth by airline over time


Data was scraped from Wikipedia using Colab, where the data was also cleaned. At the end, the cleaned data was converted to a csv file to rebase the numbers at an index of 100 in 2006 to make the trajectories more easily comparable. Airlines colored in blue signify low-cost carriers, whereas the remaining gray ones are national flag carriers.

Ranking of European Airlines and Airline groups


The data for this chart was scraped off of Wikipedia, using Python. Worth noting is that many of the large airline groups in the data comprise multiple different airlines, such as Luftansa Group, International Airline Group and AirFrance-KLM. The trends seen in the bar chart thus strongly indicate a growing demand for low-cost carriers. In 2019, Ryanair surpassed Lufthansa Group and has remained in the lead, maintaining a considerably greater margin throughout the pandemic. This is particularly interesting since the total passenger number mostly evened out during 2020 and in the recovery year, 2021.

Number of passengers carried in Europe (1996-2020)


This temporal map chart shows the number of passengers carried by European country to show the growing demand of air transport, and thus the market pie to be split across carriers. Clustering by Airline stock prices (2017-2022)

The dendrogram displays clusters of stock prices between 2017 and 2022 for eight airlines: Wizzair, Ryanair, TUI, Scandinavian Airlines, Lufthansa, Norwegian Air Shuttle, EasyJet and AirFrance-KLM. When clustering stock data for the eight airlines, I use the dynamic time warping technique to better capture similarities in trends.

The two low-cost carriers, Wizzair and Ryanair, are the most distinguishable set of airlines, as seen in the length of the upper purple branch. All the other carriers are grouped into the lower purple branch, where national flag carrier AirFrance-KLM and low-cost carrier EasyJet interestingly share more similarities in trends relative to the other airlines. The grouping of the remianing airlines are nonetheless somewhat more consistent. Neither TUI nor SAS are low-cost carriers, and these seem to ge grouped with national flag carrier Lufthansa. Evidently, the separated airline here is Norwegian Air Shuttle, a low-cost carrier. The clustering of airline types is thus not entirely clear.
